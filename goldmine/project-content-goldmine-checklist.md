## Section 1 — The Photos You Need (and Probably Don't Have)

### 1. The Before Photo
*The Most Missed Shot*

Most contractors forget this until the job is done — and by then it's gone. The before shot is the most important photo of the entire project because transformation is the story homeowners hire you for. It doesn't need to look good. In fact, ugly is better. The worse the before looks, the more impressive your after becomes.

**Why it matters:** Without a before, you just have a photo of a nice kitchen. With a before, you have proof of what you can do. Proof is what converts a homeowner who's researching contractors at 11pm.

**Action:** Set a phone reminder for the first day of every job: "Before photos — wide shot, detail shot, the problem area."

- [ ] No — I'll start tomorrow

---

### 2. The Detail Shot
*The Scroll-Stopper*

Not a wide shot. Not the whole room. One specific thing — the tile pattern, the way the cabinet doors sit flush, the trim meeting the ceiling at a perfect 45 — that shows a homeowner you care about the details most contractors rush past. Get close. Fill the frame. No wide angle.

**Why it matters:** Homeowners can't see quality from across the room. The detail shot is what makes them stop scrolling — and start wondering what you'd do in their kitchen.

- [ ] Yes, I have it
- [ ] No — I'll get it on the next job

---

### 3. The Natural Light Shot
*The One That Changes Everything*

Same project. Same phone. Open the blinds, turn off the overhead lights, wait for morning light from the window. The difference will look like you hired a photographer. Harsh overhead flash kills colors, flattens surfaces, and makes even $80K work look cheap.

**Why it matters:** Most contractor photos look the same — overlit, slightly blue, flat. One natural light photo in your feed immediately makes you look like the premium option. That distinction is worth more than any ad you could run.

**Quick test:** If you can see a reflection of the flash or ceiling light anywhere in the photo — retake it. Open a window first.

- [ ] Yes, I have it
- [ ] No — retaking my last project this week

---

### 4. The Scale Shot
*The Budget Justifier*

Something in the frame that shows a homeowner how big the job actually was. An open floor plan with furniture placed back in. A kitchen island with a chair pulled up to it. A bathroom vanity with towels on the hooks. Scale makes the project feel real — and makes the budget feel justified.

**Why it matters:** A wide shot of an empty room looks like a showroom. A shot with a human element — a stool, a plant, a towel — makes a homeowner picture themselves there. That's the moment they decide to call.

- [ ] Yes, I have it
- [ ] No — I'll stage the next one before I leave the site

---

### 5. The Progress Shot
*The Trust Builder*

The messy middle. The demo, the framing, the subfloor before tile. This is counterintuitive — why would you show homeowners the chaos? Because it proves you did the work. Any contractor can show a finished kitchen. Only a confident one shows what it took to get there.

**Why it matters:** Homeowners worry about contractors who disappear mid-job, cut corners, or hide problems. A progress photo says: we're on-site, we know what we're doing, and we have nothing to hide. That's the kind of trust that closes estimates.

- [ ] Yes, I have it
- [ ] No — taking one on my current job this week

## Section 2 — What Your Caption Needs to Say

### 6. City in the First Line
*The #1 Local SEO Move*

"Kitchen remodel in Walnut Creek" ranks in local search. "Beautiful kitchen remodel!" does not. Every caption, every platform, every single time — city first. It costs you nothing and it's the easiest thing you can do to get found by homeowners searching in your area.

**Why it matters:** When a homeowner types "kitchen remodel Danville" into Google or Instagram search, your post only shows up if those words are actually in it. Most contractors skip the city. That's your advantage — use it.

- [ ] Yes, always
- [ ] No — updating my last 5 posts today

---

### 7. Name the Challenge
*What Separates Good Captions from Great Ones*

"This kitchen had a load-bearing wall exactly where the island needed to go" is a caption. "Beautiful kitchen remodel — swipe to see the transformation!" is not. The challenge is what makes a homeowner realize this wasn't easy — and that you knew how to handle it. It's the difference between a photo and a story.

**Why it matters:** Homeowners choose contractors they believe can handle their specific problem. When you name a challenge you solved, every homeowner with a similar problem reads that and thinks: "This is the person I need to call."

**Caption formula:** [City] + [Project type] + [One specific challenge you solved] + [How you solved it]

- [ ] Yes, my captions name a challenge
- [ ] No — rewriting my last 3 captions now

---

### 8. A Specific Result
*The Line That Books Jobs*

"The homeowner now has 40% more counter space and a kitchen that finally fits how their family actually cooks" beats "the client was thrilled" every single time. Specific results make future homeowners picture themselves as your client. Vague outcomes do nothing.

**Why it matters:** When someone is deciding between two contractors, they're really deciding: "Which one gets results that matter to me?" A specific result — more storage, a brighter room, a bathroom their guests actually comment on — is the answer to that question before they even ask it.

- [ ] Yes, my captions include specific results
- [ ] No — I'm adding results language starting today

## Section 3 — Your Posting System

### 9. Your Project Library
*The One Folder That Changes Everything*

Every project gets its own folder, named by city and type: "Walnut Creek Kitchen Nov 2024." Before, during, and after — all in one place. Right now you probably have 300 photos in your camera roll with no idea which job most of them are from. That's why they never get posted.

**Why it matters:** The contractors who post consistently aren't the ones with the most time. They're the ones who can find their photos in 60 seconds. A folder system turns an overwhelming camera roll into a content library you can pull from anytime.

**Action:** Start with your last 3 jobs. Create the folders today. That's your first month of content right there.

- [ ] Yes, I have a system
- [ ] No — creating 3 folders right now

---

### 10. The Blocked Weekly Slot
*The Habit That Builds a Pipeline*

Not "when I have time." A specific day, a specific time, in your calendar as a recurring event. Tuesday at 7am. Thursday at noon. Pick one and block it like a client meeting — because that's exactly what it is. The homeowner who finds you next month is the reason you're showing up today.

**Why it matters:** Every week you don't post is a week a homeowner in Danville or San Ramon searched for a remodeler, didn't find you, and called someone else. Consistency isn't a nice-to-have. It's the difference between a pipeline and a dry spell.

**Action:** Block it right now. Open your calendar. Recurring event, weekly. Call it "Content — 20 minutes." Do it before you close this checklist.

- [ ] Yes, it's in my calendar
- [ ] No — blocking it right now
